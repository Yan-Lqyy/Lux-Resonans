Theory of Light Interference and Diffraction

Wave Nature of Light

Light exhibits properties of both waves and particles. Interference and diffraction are phenomena that strongly support the wave model of light.

Huygens' Principle

Huygens' principle states that every point on a wavefront can be considered as a source of secondary spherical wavelets. The new wavefront is the envelope of these wavelets.

Interference

When two or more waves overlap in space, the resultant displacement is the vector sum of the individual displacements. This is called the principle of superposition.

  • Constructive Interference: Occurs when waves meet in phase (e.g., crest meets crest), resulting in a wave of greater amplitude. For double slits, bright fringes occur where the path difference from the slits is an integer multiple of the wavelength: d sin(θ) = mλ, where m = 0, ±1, ±2, ...
  • Destructive Interference: Occurs when waves meet out of phase (e.g., crest meets trough), resulting in a wave of lesser (or zero) amplitude. For double slits, dark fringes occur where the path difference is a half-integer multiple of the wavelength: d sin(θ) = (m + 1/2)λ.

Diffraction

Diffraction is the bending of waves around the corners of an obstacle or through an aperture into the region of geometrical shadow of the obstacle/aperture.

Single-Slit Diffraction

When light passes through a single slit, it spreads out. The pattern observed on a screen consists of a central bright maximum, flanked by alternating dark and progressively weaker bright fringes.

The condition for minima (dark fringes) in single-slit diffraction is given by: a sin(θ) = mλ, where 'a' is the slit width, λ is the wavelength, θ is the angle of diffraction, and m = ±1, ±2, ... (m cannot be 0, as that corresponds to the central maximum).

The intensity I at an angle θ is approximately I(θ) = I₀ * (sin(β)/β)², where β = (πa / λ) * sin(θ).

Double-Slit Interference (with Diffraction effects)

When light passes through two closely spaced slits, the pattern observed is a combination of the interference pattern from two point sources and the diffraction pattern from each individual slit.

The intensity I at an angle θ is approximately I(θ) = I₀ * (sin(β)/β)² * cos²(α), where:

  • β = (πa / λ) * sin(θ) (due to diffraction from slit width 'a')
  • α = (πd / λ) * sin(θ) (due to interference from slit separation 'd')
